Homeostasis: Balance within Nature

What is homeostasis?

Homeostasis is the ability to maintain balance within an organism. In other words, it is the ability of an organism to maintain a stable internal environment, despite changes to its internal or external environment. Homeostasis is the maintenance of equilibrium (balance). Life is dependent on this balance in order to survive. Otherwise, organisms would be unsuitable for life.

Think of it this way: when you see a ball flying towards you, your instant reflex is to move away. That is homeostasis at work. Homeostasis is what is keeping you alive. The nervous system receives information from the eyes and sends messages to the joints and limbs to move away from potential harm.

This is done through the processes of feedback pathways. Feedback pathways are pathways that the body can take when experiencing a certain stimulus (driving force or factor). There are two pathways the body may take: negative feedback pathway and positive feedback pathway. Negative feedback is the more common form. An example of it would be maintaining internal body temperature. When the body is too hot, the body will try to lower its temperature until it reaches the optimal range. The main goal of it is to stabilize the body back to its original state. On the other hand, positive feedback works in opposite to negative feedback. One example of it would be giving brith to a baby. Contractions of the uterus causes hormones (chemical signals) to be released to cause further contractions until the baby is released.

Both negative feedback and positive feedback are important in maintaining homeostasis because they help the body reach a balanced state efficiently.

Nervous System


One important system that regulates homeostasis is the nervous system. The nervous system acts as a central coordinating system, sending and receiving signals to make decisions based on what is happening to the body. The nervous system consists of two parts: the central nervous system and the peripheral nervous system.

The central nervous system (CNS) consists of the brain and spinal cord. This can be thought of as the main office of the company. The brain is where decisions are made and sent out, while the spinal cord can be thought of as the main hall leading to the office. On its way to and from the office, there are many doors that lead to separate rooms and workplaces. These rooms and workplaces can be thought of as the peripheral nervous system (PNS). The peripheral nervous systems works with the central nervous system to get work done. It receives and sends signals to the brain, similar to how workers in a company receive orders from the head and report back. The main function of the PNS is to connect the CNS to the limbs, organs and skin.

Together, the nervous system works to maintain balance within the body based on external or internal factors. These factors can include a change in surrounding temperature, physical harm and potential threats.

Central Nervous System


The CNS consist of two vital organs: the brain and spinal cord. The brain is the main coordinating system and plays a crucial role in controlling most of the bodily functions such asÂ movements, sensations, thoughts, speech, and memory.


The spinal cord connects a part of the brain called the brainstem and runs through the spinal canal. It acts as a pathway between the brain and peripheral nervous system to carry signals back and forth.

The cerebrum is the largest part of the brain. It is responsible for controlling voluntary actions such as speech, senses, thought and memory. The cerebrum can be further divided into two halves: the right and left hemispheres.

Each hemisphere divides into four regions called lobes. These include:

Frontal lobes - Located in the front of the brain and they are responsible for skills such as voluntary movement, speech output and organizational skills.

Parietal lobes - Located behind the frontal lobes and they process sensory information. This information can include temperature, taste and touch.

Temporal lobes - Located on each side of the brain and are responsible for processing memory as well as processing auditory information from hearing.

Occipital lobes - Located at the back of the brain and are responsible for processing or receiving visual information from sight.

Another very important structure of the brain is the cerebellum. It is located near the brainstem which is the area connecting the brain to the spinal cord. The cerebellum is responsible for many functions including motor skills such as coordination, balance and posture.



The hypothalamus is a small region located near the base of the brain. It plays a crucial role in regulating automatic functions including thirst, appetite and body temperature (Fernando, 2020). It also plays an important role in working with other body systems to maintain homeostasis (which will be discussed later). It secretes hormones based on different internal or environmental influences such as puberty.
Hormones are chemical signals that are capable of activating certain functions when received by specific organs.

The pituitary gland is located under the hypothalamus at the base of the brain. It is responsible for producing hormones that are capable of controlling many functions which are processed by the endocrine system (another body system; responsible for regulating hormones). The pituitary gland regulates the production of hormones and works with the endocrine system to accomplish balance.

First, let us define a few key structures.

Neuron/Nerve Cell - A specialized cell within the body that works specifically for the nervous system. It uses chemical and electrical signals to communicate with other neurons/nerve cells in order for messages to be passed on to organs, tissues and the brain. In other words, it sends and receives messages. The term neuron is often used interchangeable with nerve cell. The signals passed between these cells are called synapses.



The diagram to the left illustrates the typical structure of a neuron. Dendrites are the extended branches reaching out from the cell body. Dendrites are specialized to receive chemical signals from other neurons. These chemical signals are converted into small electrical impulses and sent inward toward the cell body. The nucleus is found in the centre of the cell body and it stores the cellâs genetic material necessary for coding proteins. The axon is the long section that branches away from the cell body. Electrical impulses travel down this path and leave the neuron through the axon terminal. The myelin sheath wraps around the axon and protects the nerves from other electrical impulses. It also poses to transmit electrical impulses more quickly and efficiently. Schwann cells are a type of glial cell (another type of cell that helps the nervous system) that help to form the myelin sheath. The Nodes of Ranvier are the gaps between the myelin sheath that speedup action potentials.



What are action potentials? Recall that the body contains many chemicals in the form of charged molecules (also known as ions). Likewise, electrical and chemical signals sent between neurons are just temporary shifts between positive and negative sides which we call action potentials. The outer membrane of a neuron is semi-permeable, meaning only some substances can enter. Thus allowing only certain ions to enter. Neurons are negatively charged when at rest, while the outside the neuron is more positive. When neurons receive chemical signals in the form of ions, there is a sudden flow of positive ions through the neuron. This causes the neuron to become more positive until the ions flow out of the neuron. The shift between positive and negative is referred to as an action potential and it is responsible for creating electrical impulses when ions enter the neuron.

Nervous System and Endocrine System

The nervous system and endocrine system work together and play a vital role in maintaining homeostasis. The hypothalamus (part of the brain) releases hormones after receiving signals of a change or inbalance. These hormones eventually reach the pituitary gland (part of the nervous system and endocrine system). In the response to these hormones, the pituitary gland then secretes its own hormones which are picked up by target organs. The organs receive these hormones and undergo a certain function as a result. In short, the hypothalamus recieves signals from the nervous system and sends messages (in the form of hormones) to the pituitary gland to release its own hormones in response.

One example is the use of hormones to influence blood sugar levels.
When sugar levels are high in the blood, a hormone called insulin is released, whereas a hormone called glucagon is released when the sugar levels are low. The hypothalamus receives signals from the nervous system that notify it that sugar levels are either too high or too low. This causes it to release hormones which trigger the pituitary gland to secrete its own hormones. The hormones from the pituitary gland cause the pancreas to release insulin or glucagon in response to it. These two hormones then regulate the amount of sugar in the blood.

Another example is the role of hormones in puberty. Once again, the hypothalamus receives signals from the nervous system and lets it know when it is time for puberty. This causes the hypothalamus to secrete a hormone called gonadotropin (GnRH). GnRH causes the pituitary gland to secrete hormones LH and FSH. These hormones are present in both males and females. The only difference is the amount present. In males, more LH is present and it encourages the production of testosterone. For females, more FSH is present and it encourages the production of estrogen. Both testosterone and estrogen act as hormones to influence the development of further growth characteristics.





Nervous System and Circulatory System

The nervous system and circulatory system complement each other in their work and function. Just like all cells in the body, neurons or glial cells require oxygen to release energy. The presence of oxygen is crucial for cells to get the energy they need to function. Otherwise, they would not be able to perform their duties to support the body. The circulatory system helps the nervous system cells and all other cells to get the oxygen needed to perform functions. The blood circulates to all parts of the body where cells need oxygen.

In turn, the nervous system monitors blood pressure and heart rate. The blood must sustain a certain level of pressure in order for glucose (sugar) in the blood to be efficiently reabsorbed though the kidneys (we will touch on this shortly). Inability to reabsorb glucose results in a loss of nutrients. As for heart rate, the nervous system lowers and raises heart rate in response to certain stimuli (factors). For instance, a dangerous or stressful situation can cause the brain to increase heart rate. This would increase the rate of blood flowing around the body as a defence mechanism to get more oxygen to cells so they can produce more energy. Higher energy production would act as a defence against possible threats to the body such as physical harm.






Nervous System and Excretory System

The excretory system is also commonly referred to as the urinary system. The nervous system and excretory system work together to dispose harmful wastes from the body. When the time is right, the bladder sends signals to the brain letting it know that it is time for disposal. The brain then controls the process of urination by sending signals to bladder muscles to release their grip and lighten. This causes urine to leave the bladder and exit the body through the urethra. However, before this can happen, the urine must be filtered in order for nutrients and water to be reabsorbed before it can leave the body as urine. This is where the kidneys come into play. Waste is filtered through the kidneys to reabsorb any remaining glucose or excess water to maximize the nutrients gained. After this, the urine can be stored in the bladder, awaiting the right time to be released.


What does this have to do with homeostasis? Well, waste products are toxic and and can harm the body. In many organisms, waste comes in the form of urine, which contains a level of highly acidic uric acid. This is harmful to the body and strays it from balance if not dealt with. However, this balance is maintained through homeostasis by nervous system and endocrine system regulation. They work together to dispose harmful substances.

Reflexes

Reflexes are regulated by simple connections called reflex arcs. Picture this: you are walking by a stove and your arm accidentally brushes over it. Your first instinct is to move your arm away. Why is this?

Reflex arcs are activated when we experience sudden danger. This allows us to respond to the situation in the form of reflexes. Reflexes are involuntary actions that would otherwise take too long for the voluntary portion of the brain to process. A reflex arc moves directly to and from the brain or spinal cord before the voluntary portion of the brain can even process this sensory information. The voluntary portion of the brain processes the information after/during the reflex which is why your first instinct is to move away. Typically, there are only three types of neurons that are involved in this process:

â¢ Sensory neurons - Senses a potentially harmful stimulus (factor).
â¢ Interneurons - Activated by sensory neurons in the spinal cord.
â¢ Motor neurons - Instructs muscles to withdraw or evaluate from the harmful stimulus.

Sensory neurons sense certain changes and stimuli, then send signals to interneurons in the spinal cord. Interneurons form a bridge between the central nervous system and the sensory neurons. However, before the messages can be processed by the brain, motor neurons receive response from the spinal cord to perform a reflex action. The brain fully processes the information after/during the reflex.

Reflexes allow organisms to move away from potential danger without receiving messages directly from the voluntary portion of the brain which would otherwise take too long. The time it takes for a message to be received and sent via the brain may delay the bodyâs response by a given amount of time. This would increase the chance of damage to the body. Delayed reactions hinder the bodyâs response and tend away from homeostasis. Thus reflexes support homeostasis by decreasing the time needed to perform a certain response and protecting the body in a more efficient way.

Blood Sugar Levels

In an earlier section, blood sugar levels were discussed briefly. Now let us dive into the heart of it. As you may have learned, the blood contains a varying level of glucose (sugar) found within it. The stomach breaks down food into this sugar and it is absorbed via the blood stream. The blood also reabsorbs this sugar from the kidneys or wherever reabsorption is possible. Thus influencing the amount of sugar in the blood. When we refer to sugar levels, we are referring to the amount of sugar found within an individualâs blood. A normal range is a specified by a considerate amount that would not cause harm to the body.

However, there are instances when the blood sugar level is too high or too low. Above the normal range is known as hyperglycaemia and below the healthy range is known as hypoglycaemia. This is where the nervous system comes into play. The autonomic nervous system (regulates involuntary actions) monitors the levels of sugar in the blood. When levels are too low, perhaps from exercising, the automatic nervous system signals the pancreas to secrete a hormone called glucagon. Glucagon is a hormone that encourages more glucose to be released into the blood.

When the sugar level is too high, perhaps from digestion of food, the autonomic nervous system signals the pancreas to secrete another hormone called insulin. Insulin is a hormone that encourages less glucose to be in the blood. It makes it easier for cells to absorb and converts the sugar into a more storable molecule called glycogen.



Regulating blood glucose levels is an example of a negative feedback loop.
Recall: A negative feedback loop is a pathway taken when the body experiences an imbalance internally or externally and tries to immediately stabilize back to its original state.

Quick Note: The pancreas is an organ in the body responsible for producing enzymes (speeds up chemical reactions) for digestion and producing hormones to maintain sugar levels.

High and low blood sugar levels could cause harm to the body through increased chance of a heart attack or heart failure and even cancer. High blood sugar level (hyperglycaemia) is especially dangerous for people with diabetes since they cannot lower it on their own. Therefore, high and low blood sugar level can cause harm to the body and be potentially life-threatening. The nervous system maintains homeostasis by regulating the levels of sugar so it remains in a healthy range.






Emotions

The main parts of the nervous system that contributes to the nature of emotions is the autonomic nervous system and hypothalamus.

The hypothalamus regulates the autonomic nervous system which controls various processes and glands. These include: pulse, blood pressure, breathing and arousal to emotional circumstances. The hypothalamus is also involved in emotional processing. Possibly, the hypothalamus senses certain stimuli in the environment which causes it to react and signal the autonomic nervous system.

Quick Refresher: The autonomic nervous system is divided into two divisions - sympathetic nervous system and parasympathetic nervous system.

In response to hormones from the hypothalamus, the sympathetic nervous system causes adrenal glands to release a hormone called adrenaline (also referred to as epinephrine). This causes the fight-or-flight response. This response then increases blood flow to muscles and an increased heart rate. Increased blood flow and heart rate enables the body to move quickly and feel less pain if perceived as dangerous. Emotions of anger or stress are susceptible to this state.

Here is an example: When the body encounters a stressful situation (for example, a presentation), the short-term stress response may be activated. The hypothalamus in the brain receives messages and releases hormones in response. These hormones then reach the adrenal medulla (inside the adrenal glands) to signal them to release the hormone adrenaline. Adrenaline encourages the increase of blood flow, heart rate, blood pressure and metabolism (chemical reactions in the body). The main reason for this is to allow the body to have a burst of energy. The body may use this energy to protect itself from potential danger. However, in regards to stressful situations, this energy is translated into feelings of restlessness from the short-term stress response. The fight-or-flight response may be activated and thus may cause the body to try to flee from potential danger to protect itself.

On the other side, the parasympathetic nervous system functions when the body is at rest. It helps the body store energy and decreases blood flow to muscles. Thus giving the body a more calmed and relaxed state.

The sympathetic nervous system and parasympathetic nervous system work in partnership to maintain homeostasis by functioning like switches. When one division is functioning, the other is at rest and vice versa. For instance, the body may sense a particular stimulus that could harm the body so the nervous system may respond to it by increasing heart rate so feelings of anger can protect the body. The action itself is a defence mechanism to protect the body. On the contrary, when everything is normal, the nervous system has no need to maintain a high heart rate so it lowers it to be more efficient and use less energy. This action is mainly used to help the body store energy. Recent research has even shown that sadness may be portrayed by the nervous system as another defence mechanism. Crying has been suggested to release oxytocin and endorphins which are chemical messengers that the body may use to help relieve stress (Legg, 2020). The hypothalamus complements both divisions in maintaining homeostasis by regulating them based on surroundings.




Body temperature

The nervous system is also responsible for regulating the internal temperature of the body. The average human has a body temperature between 37 degrees Celsius and 37.8 degrees Celsius. However, if the the body temperature falls below 35 degrees Celsius, that is considered hypothermia (below the healthy range). This can can lead to cardiac arrest, brain damage or possibly death. If the body temperature rises above 42 degrees Celsius, that is considered hyperthermia (above healthy range). This can lead to brain damage and even death (Holland, 2017).

Several factors influence temperature change.
For raise in temperature, it may include: fever, exercise and digestion.
For drop in temperature, it may include: drug use and alcohol use

The hypothalamus senses changes in body temperature and sends signals to muscles, organs and glands to respond in various ways.

When the body needs to cool down:

â¢ Sweating occurs, the hypothalamus sends signals to sweat glands to release sweat. Sweating cools the skin as it evaporates and helps lower internal temperature.
â¢ Blood vessels under skin get wider. Signals from the hypothalamus tell blood vessels to relax. This allows for more blood to flow to cooler spots and away from the inner body. Heat can be released as a result.

When the body needs to warm up:

â¢ Blood vessels under skin become narrower. The hypothalamus sends signals to blood vessels to constrict. This causes blood flow under the skin to decrease and retain more heat in the inner body.
â¢ Hormones are released to increase metabolism. The hypothalamus can send signals to the thyroid glands to produce hormones which will increase metabolism. More energy and heat is produced as a result.

Dramatic change in body temperature from the healthy range could put the body at risk for many health concerns and be life-threatening. Thus straying the body from homeostasis. The nervous system regulates the internal temperature of the body and keeps it in a healthy range to prevent homeostasis imbalance.




Vision

The nervous system plays a role in visual perception of images and maintaining homeostasis as well. In humans, nervous tissue called the retina covers the rear inside of the eye. This tissue contains millions of light-detecting cells that receive the optic information and transmits it to the brain through the optic nerve (Editors, 2018). This results in a visual picture. The dilation of the pupils is also controlled by the nervous system. Dilation of the pupils allows the eyes to optimize the right amount of light entering for the best vision. Thus getting a clear image. Nerves in the eyes send this sensory information to the brain so an image can be processed.

Here is a more detailed look: Light enters the eyes through the pupils. It controls how much light enters by dilating in response to the amount of light available. Less light and the pupils dilate larger, more light and the pupils may dilate smaller. The shape of the lens is altered by muscles behind the iris so farther/near images can be brought into focus on the retina. The retina is located near the back of the eye and contains millions of photoreceptors that detect light. Since humans have two eyes, this allows us to have binocular vision. Basically, the ability to perceive an image using both eyes. Visual information from each eye is passed to the optic nerve to the optic chiasm where some nerve fibres cross over. This cross over allows both sides of the brain to receive signals from both eyes. As a result, the right side of the brain processes information from the left side of the scene while the left side of the brain processes information from the right side of the scene.

There are two types of photoreceptors (specialized neurons) called rods and cones. Rods are sensitive to light and allow us to see in dim light, but they do not convey colour. On the other hand, cones are responsible for colour and acute detail. The human eye contains three types of cones: red, blue and green. Each one is sensitive to a different range of colours. Contrary to that, the human eye can detect thousands of colours since the sensitivities overlap. This overlap allows colours to appear in a variety of ways.

The centre part of the retina where light is focused is called the fovea. The area around it is called the macula and it is critical for reading and driving. Death of photoreceptors in the macula can cause blindness because light in the area cannot be focused or detected.

Management of vision allows humans and organisms to get a better perception of the world around them. Better vision allows organisms to detect potential threats. For example, humans can associate specific colours with certain meanings such as colourful berries that are poisonous. Therefore, better vision helps maintain homeostasis by having more consciousness to surroundings.

Impact

If the nervous system was unable to perform its role in maintaining homeostasis, the body would be unable to keep an organism alive. Think about it this way, the nervous system works with many other organ systems and collaborates with a number of organs. Without it, the body would not know how to function effectively. Other systems would not work efficiently and even cause many problems.

On the internal level (inside the body), many problems would occur. The bladder (stores waste until it is released) would not know when to release urine (waste) and there could be a build-up of harmful chemicals as a result. Also, heart regulation could easily become abnormal and lead to strokes (reduced amount of blood to the brain) or heart attacks (insufficient oxygen reaches the heart). The nervous system would leave the other systems defenceless and out of control.

On the external level, the nervous system is responsible for our senses. These include: sight, hearing, touch, smell and taste. Essentially, the body would lose all of these senses. Thus making the organism unconscious to its surroundings. Without consciousness, an organism would basically have no perception of the world around them. This could be harmful on the external level. No perception would mean that an organism has no awareness of what is happening. Furthermore, life would be impossible.

On both levels, homeostasis would be unsustainable.

Epilepsy

Epilepsy is a long-term condition in which a person experiences repeated seizures. Seizures are caused when electrical activity in the brain is disrupted. Depending on the part of the brain involved, the symptoms of seizures can vary. These may include: loss of consciousness and unusual feelings or behaviours.

It is important to note that epilepsy is not just one condition. Many conditions can lead to prolonged seizures. There are many known types of seizures such as generalized seizures which affect the whole brain and focal seizures which affect only part of the brain.

In general, epilepsy can be caused by damage to the brain such as a head injury, stroke, brain diseases or even from genetic factors. Not much is known about the causes, however, it is known that oneâs family history can play a major role.

Epilepsy affects the nervous systemâs ability to maintain homeostasis because it leaves the body vulnerable when seizures occur. For example, a seizure may cause an individual to lose consciousness and accidentally trip. The signals sent within the brain are disrupted depending on which region is affected. This causes that region or the whole brain to lose control over the body for a given period.

Typically, Parkinsonâs disease may occur in people over the age of 65, though it can vary. The symptoms of this disease include: balance problems, slowing of movement, stooped posture, muscle stiffness or shaking which usually begins in one arm/hand.

Currently, there is no cure and doctors are not sure exactly what causes this. Perhaps it could be exposure to specific toxins or hereditary (though only a small portion of patients are shown to have this connection).

Parkinsonâs disease affects the body's dopamine levels. Dopamine influences signals in the brain. Damage to that region would affect how dopamine is used and signals may be lost. Therefore, this would affect the nervous system's ability to maintain homeostasis because of signals that are unsuccessfully sent. Overall, the nervous system cannot maintain homeostasis because nerves in that region may be unresponsive. Thus interfering with an individualâs motor skills and putting them at risk of potential danger. Unresponsive nerves can result in loss or decreased ability to facilitate movement.





Multiple Sclerosis (MS)

As you may recall, the myelin sheath is a structure that wraps around the axon of a neuron.

Here is a brief video to provide a refresh on neuron structure. Neuron Refresh

Multiple sclerosis is a disease in which the myelin sheath of the neurons in the brain and spinal cord are damaged. The cause of this is unknown but many researchers are theorizing that it may be an autoimmune disease.

Note: An autoimmune disease is a condition in which the immune system mistakenly attacks the body.

Others theorize it may be caused by genetics or environmental factors or from a virus.

Symptoms of multiple sclerosis may include:

â¢ Problems controlling the body such as loss of coordination and balance
â¢ Changes in memory, concentration, reasoning, emotions or mood
â¢ Tiredness or insensitivity to heat

Overall, multiple sclerosis causes signals traveling through neurons to pass slower or less smoothly. Since the myelin sheath allows electrical impulses to be transmitted quickly and efficiently, the process of impulse flow is less effective compared to having undamaged myelin sheath. This can delay or affect signals and potentially leave the body with a delayed/disrupted reaction. Also, the myeline sheath protects the nerves from other electrical impulse, acting like an insulating layer on a cable. If it is damaged, electrical impulses could damage nerves as well. Therefore, multiple sclerosis affects the body's ability to maintain homeostasis due to delayed/disrupted messages which can affect bodily reactions and cause potential harm of nerves. This leaves the individual at risk and with an imbalance which could cause harm if untreated.
